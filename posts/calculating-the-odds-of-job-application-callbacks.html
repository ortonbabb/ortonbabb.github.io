Calculating the Odds of Job Application Callbacks

July 5, 2020 - 3 minute read -
Python Career

In this post, I try to give a data-driven answer to the question of how many job applications one would need to send to get a virtual guarantee of a job offer.

Background

“In recruiting, we have what is known as a ‘hiring funnel’ or yield model for every job which helps recruiting leaders understand how many total applications they need to generate in order to get a single hire. As an applicant, this funnel reveals your chances of success at each step of the hiring process. For the specific case of an online job posting, on average, 1,000 individuals will see a job post, 200 will begin the application process, 100 will complete the application, 75 of those 100 resumes will be screened out by either the ATS or a recruiter, 25 resumes will be seen by the hiring manager, 4 to 6 will be invited for an interview, 1 to 3 of them will be invited back for final interview, 1 will be offered that job and 80 percent of those receiving an offer will accept it.”
– Pat Smith for Aviation Jobs Blog

Code

We can use these values to answer the question. As always, I am using Python to do this, so first, let’s import the necessary libraries.

import numpy as np
import plotly.express as px
import plotly.io as pio

Next, define the relevant variables. We use hacker statistics to estimate the PMF by sampling 10,000 times from a binomial distribution with 200 Bernoulli trials and probability of success of 3%.

numApplications = 200
callBackRate = 0.03
samples = np.random.binomial(numApplications, callBackRate, size = 10000)

We can plot a histogram with plot.ly,

fig = px.histogram(x=samples, labels={'x': 'Number of Callbacks'}, histnorm='probability density', title="PMF Given 200 Job Apps with 3% Callback Rate")
fig.show()
pio.write_html(fig, file='numberofcallbacks.html',auto_open=True)

Suppose you think you need to interview at least 4 times to get at least one solid offer. We can calculate the odds of getting at least 4 or more callbacks.

minCallbacks = 4
print(f'The chance of getting {minCallbacks} or more callbacks would be {'%.2f' % (100 * np.sum(trials >= minCallbacks) / 10000))}%')

This reported the odds to be 85.10%. The calculation assumes a conservative callback rate and that the applicant casts a wide net. Since over 200 applications are required to get a virtual guarantee (>90%), we vindicate the suspicion that blind-applying is more or less a numbers game!
