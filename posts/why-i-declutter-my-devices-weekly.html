Why I Declutter My Devices Weekly

March 27, 2020 - 2 minute read -
Self-help

Nowadays, digital decluttering should be on par with other house chores. It allows easy access to the files we need, reduces distractions, and makes using our devices, overall, a more pleasing experience.

Digital decluttering helps us to find files when we need them.

While it’s harder to let belongings gather up in our physical space, it’s easy to have dozens of documents piling up in our Desktop or Downloads folder. Many of these items are byproducts of day to day tasks that are no longer needed. By taking the time to delete what is unnecessary and store what needs to be referenced later in an appropriate folder, I’ve saved myself a lot of stress.

Digital decluttering reduces distractions.

Have you ever gone on the computer to do something pressing only to get sidetracked by the 79544678 tabs you had left open? It can be easy to leave windows open now that computers pretty much never need restarting. But would you leave last month’s newspaper lying around in the middle of the living room? I hope not.

Decluttering every week makes using our computers and smartphones enjoyable, refreshing even.

It feels like I’m using a new device every time I log on. During the week, I’m often taking snapshots that I need but won’t use in the future. At events and on trips, I also take lots of photos. Most of them will not turn out well. When I’m trying to show someone an old picture, it can be awkward having to sift through the rest.

Of course, it’s disappointing to discover that I’ve discarded something I should have kept. I just back up all that stuff from my phone on my computer.

So far, I’ve hinted at places where it makes sense to do some digital housekeeping, but here is a more extensive list:

  • Desktop and Downloads
  • Smartphone photos
  • E-mail subscriptions
  • Music I haven’t listened to in ages
  • Accounts on platforms I don’t use
  • Apps I don’t use, or that send too many notifications
  • Safari reading lists, bookmarks or similar
  • Online shopping wishlists
  • Social media pages whose content is no longer relevant to me

At heart, having easy access to the files, reducing distractions, and making space to enjoy devices are the benefits of my approach. The attitude towards technology I am describing here is reminiscent of Cal Newport’s Digital Minimalism, which extols the virtues of an intentional relationship with our devices. I’ve listened to him speak and read a summary of his book. Digital minimalism emphasizes being present in the moment and minimizing passive screen time. Digital decluttering fits in with that as well.
