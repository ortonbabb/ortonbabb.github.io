Job Search Sprints

July 13, 2020 - 2 minute read -
Career

In Calculating the Odds of Job Application Callbacks, I did some simulations to estimate that, given a 3% callback rate and 25% success rate for interviews, I would have to submit at least 200 job applications to guarantee one or more job offers. In my research on Career Design, I confirmed that finding the best jobs requires both internet mining and networking due to the existence of an unpublished job market, referral programs and the need to learn more about roles. Now that I’m job searching aggressively, I’ve planned something like a Scrum Sprint that I will use to reach the application goal in under 100 hours of labor distributed over one month.

My goal is to submit 200 job applications over 20 working days (roughly one month). The availability of jobs primarily constrains the possibilities for the job search sprint. In Filtering Entry Level-Jobs using regex, I described a script that can filter jobs from a job search board API. Given this existing infrastructure mentioned above, finding the jobs that meet pre-established criteria and preparing the application is easy.

The sprint requires an application rate of 10 posts/day. Each day will have a job search time of three to four hours. The first time block (daily scrum) is used to collect the jobs and can last up to 1 hour. It’s essential that I have verified the posts and that there won’t be any surprises at application time. In other words, I need to make sure that I have ten jobs and not, e.g., six good and four bad which would mean I have to go back and collect more. For the remaining two to three hours, I will have five blocks of about 30 minutes (the Pomodoro technique—25 minutes of work, 5 minutes breaks). In each block, I complete two applications. All the applications are recorded in a spreadsheet for review and backlogging.

There are two primary sources of jobs. The first is jobs I have collected using my custom filters, and second, those obtained using filters by job search boards such as LinkedIn, Glassdoor, and Indeed. Of the latter sources, the best is LinkedIn Premium. The premium service helps to match my skills with the post requirements and estimates how high in the applicant pool I would be. I can further rely on InMail to communicate with recruiters and also leverage my network to meet people who have worked in similar roles at a given company.

The sprint plan appears to be a reliable means of landing some offers but will be strained by the current recession. I will be updating the overall approach to respond to new information as I go along, ensuring that I stay on track. After the one month period is complete, I can come back and assess how well it worked. Let’s see how it goes!
